# Reference Observation — Reference-first mimic test v0.2

Created: 2026-06-12
Method: references decomposed at a structural level from their well-known design
language. No source code, copy, imagery, fonts, colours, or layout copied. No
reference screenshots saved into this project.

Deliberately a **different visual language from v0.1** (no riso / no registration
offset / no newsprint / no giant-type-only / no print-show ledger). v0.1's weak
points to beat: "lower half too tidy" and "only the top is strong."

## 1. Candidates (real, strong, photo / archive / editorial — not SaaS)

### A. Atlas of Places — atlasofplaces.com  ★ lead
- **Why good:** A research archive of photography, architecture, maps and film.
  Image-led, very generous whitespace, strict left-aligned editorial type, a
  numbered index/finding-aid, captions carry real weight. No card UI — items read
  as catalogued plates in a quiet study collection.
- **Borrow:** photograph-as-primary; finding-aid / numbered index; long captions
  with maker + date + medium; calm but asymmetric whitespace; restraint.
- **Don't borrow:** their exact grid, type, monochrome scheme, any images, copy.

### B. The Public Domain Review — publicdomainreview.org  (annotation reference)
- **Why good:** Editorial built *around* public-domain images, with marginal notes,
  captions and an essayistic, annotated voice. Images are cropped and placed into
  text, not dumped into a gallery grid.
- **Borrow:** annotation / marginalia voice; image embedded as evidence with a note;
  honest source/credit treatment; a single restrained accent (curatorial red).
- **Don't borrow:** their masthead, fonts, article copy, exact layout.

### C. Cleveland Museum of Art — Open Access / "collection online"  (object + source)
- **Why good:** Object-as-specimen on neutral storage ground, shelf/accession data,
  technique lines, zoomable detail. Also the actual CC0 source of the images here.
- **Borrow:** accession/shelf-mark + technique caption pattern; object photographed
  on neutral ground feel; detail call-outs.
- **Don't borrow:** their UI chrome, navigation, branding.

## 2. Chosen lead + blend

Lead: **Atlas of Places** (photo-led archive / finding-aid composition).
Annotation voice: **Public Domain Review**. Object/caption grammar + the real
CC0 images: **Cleveland Museum of Art Open Access**.

## 3. Decomposition of the lead (what generic AI LP lacks)

- **First view:** NOT a centred hero and NOT a headline. A large dark photograph
  bleeds off the top-right; the title sits low-left in open paper beside it. The
  *image* is the hero.
- **Image handling:** prints are hand-placed at different sizes, lightly rotated,
  some bleeding off the page edge; thin keyline + mount shadow + corner ticks make
  them read as physical objects on a table, not web tiles.
- **Whitespace:** intentionally uneven — large empty bone-paper zones on one side,
  dense caption/marginalia clusters on the other. Captions sometimes sit away from
  their image (finding-aid behaviour).
- **Captions / annotation:** every plate has a shelf mark, real maker/date/medium,
  an honest CC0 credit, and numbered red call-outs pointing at details, plus
  scattered marginal curator-notes.
- **Typography:** elegant high-contrast serif (titles) + neutral grotesque (labels)
  + mono (catalogue data). Not a display-type stunt — type serves the objects.
- **Background:** flat warm bone paper + faint grain. No dark gradient, no glow.
- **Grid-breaking:** a 12-col sheet that is deliberately under-filled and staggered;
  rotations, bleeds, off-grid marginalia.
- **Information density:** dense where the catalogue data lives, empty where the eye
  rests — the opposite of an even card grid.
- **vs generic AI LP:** photographs do the work, the page behaves like a finding aid
  / viewing room, and the material (paper, grain, mounts, ticks, red pencil) runs
  from the first plate to the credits — not just a strong top.
